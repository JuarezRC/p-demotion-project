\section{Introduction}
A group of South American languages from the Gran Chaco region, belonging to the Guaycuruan family, possess a set of derivational verb markers co-expressing two opposite functions, closely related to the domain of antipassive and causative. While this co-expression pattern is well-know for scholars working on Guaycuruan and neighboring Gran Chaco languages \citep[see, among many other][]{carpio2012a,Censabella2008,censabella2002,Gonzalez2015,gualdieri1998,carrio2009}, little is know about this phenomenon outside the South American context. Scholars working on those derivational markers show differences on the way that the definitional parameters related to these markers are weighed. There is, however, a recurrent observation on that the valency-decreasing, antipassive-like function primarily serves to indicate the agentivity salience and increase of the subject argument. Such a view comes up, for example, in the different glosses and segmentation proposed for cognate forms in Guaycuruan languages. A by-product of the increase in agentivity of the subject argument is the backgrounding of the former object argument. This paper explores agent foregrounding and its conditioning of object argument expression in Mocoví, a Guaycuruan language spoken in northeastern Argentina. I follow the term agent foregrounding as a language-specific label, which fits and coherently integrates to the more broad derivational system of valence-changing markers in Mocoví (and Guaycuruan). This work combines a qualitative and quantitave analysis of agent foregrounding constructions.

Goals
\begin{itemize}
\item Ways of demoting the object: explain the derivational mechanisms and show the coherence on subject orientation. 
\item Possible expression types.
\item Frequency at the discourse level and correlations with properties observed for similar constructions in the domain of antipassives. 
\item Implications of the derivational mechanism for lexicon building and other strategies at the word level of lexical derivation. Point to some comparative aspects with Mataguayan languages \citep[see][]{Nercesian2015b}.
\end{itemize}

 Following a traditional view on clause classification, here I define types of clauses by the valence profile of their predicates  \citep{Dryer2007,Mithun2005,Mithun2005a} and  their (morpho)syntactic patterning. This latter feature is in turn recognized by the way in which arguments are treated with respect to verb indexing, word order and oblique flagging. \citep[see][3-4]{comrie.etal2015}. The (morpho-)syntactic pattern of a clause will then serve to recognize clause types such as intransitive and transitive clauses, among other language-specific subtypes. From a typological angle, it is often mentioned that certain classes of predicates are taken to be present in almost all languages, namely mono-valent and bi-valent predicates, whereas  other types of predicates are subject to more language-specific variation, e.g. avalent predicates \citep[][]{Malchukov2010}. Mocoví, and to a large extent its Guaycuruan sister languages, is distinctive in that predicates are lexically categorized as either mono-valent or bi-valent and that these predicate fall into different morphosyntactic patterns. In what follows the major clause types relevant for this study are described.  

Let us begin with typical one-place predicates. Examples of this type of predicate are \textit{-oʔon} `get married' or \textit{-inta} `be similar' in (\ref{ex:clause_intrans}). In predicates of this class. the main argument expression is the bound person form, which encodes the  single argument (S). Co-nominals or pronominal elements of S arguments tend to be overt only for referent identification. Less relevant for this paper are verbless or non-verbal clauses like \textit{jim ɢoɾatom}, where the juxtaposition of elements suffice for a predication.  

\begin{exe}
 
 \ex One-place predicate clause

 \trans ‘... I got married, but I was not the same, I was very poor...' \hfill (mocCA190924\_1: ) \label{ex:clause_intrans}
\end{exe}

\subsection{The expression of P arguments: nominal determiners and their uses}

The closed class of six nominal determiners encodes different semantic values of the nominal referent, showing a clustering of salient typological features \citep[][178-181]{Aikhenvald2000}. These semantic features include presence vs. absence, position (lying, standing or sitting) and movement/proximity (coming to the speaker or away from the speaker).

In addition to the semantic features mentioned above, nominal determiners contribute to the definiteness of nominal referents. In this regards, defined and highly identifiable nouns follow  nominal determiners, as in (\ref{ex:determiner_p_ka}). Furthermore, the selection of determiners does not seem to obey exclusively to the semantic properties of the nominal referent, as discourse factors also appear to play a role. Note in the example below, for example, that the selection of the nominal determiner could have been \textit{ɲi}, as referents of this kind are commonly conceived in a sitting position, the speaker selected \textit{ka} instead, which encodes absence of the referent. This nominal determiner fits the discourse context, as the speaker narrates events from his childhood.  

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:determiner_p_ka}
\gll sa-s-awan-qa-pe-ge-a \textbf{ka} \textbf{malaʔ} \\
{\Neg}-{\First.\II}-see-{\Pl}-{\Prog}-{\LocThree}-{\Obj.\Arg} {\DetOne} bed \\
\trans `...we didn't know the bed...' \hfill(mocCA191122\_2: 00:01:48) 
\end{exe}

However, depending on the referent and, to some extent on the predicate type, nominal determiners can be omitted, which also affects the referential properties of the noun. Example (\ref{ex:no_determiner}) shows that the noun \textit{toːɾta} occurs without a nominal determiner in the eating event. The reference here is not to a single entity, but to multiple entities, which is reinforced by the dinamicity of the eating event in its progressive form. Change in the referentiality of determined and undetermined nouns is more obvious if we compare the expression of A and P arguments. Whereas the former occurs with the determiner \textit{so} and the referent is a single, individualized entity, the latter lacks a determiner and its referentiality is less individualized.
 
\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:no_determiner}
\gll \textbf{so} ɾeɢat ∅-keʔe-tak \textbf{toːɾta}{\Sploan}  kemaɾ-aɢa-j\\
{\DetTwo} jaguar {\Third\II}-eat-{\Prog} bread be\_full-{\Nmlz}-{\Attr}-{\F}\\

\trans \hfill (mocCA191127\_2: 00:09:18)

Lastly, it is worth mentioning that nominal determiners have a broader range of uses and constitute of the devices available in the language for discourse linking. Of particular relevance for this work is their clause-combining function, where they  introduce  a noun-modifying clause similar to relative clauses. This property of nominal determiner is not exclusive to Mocoví, as it has also been reported for other Guaycuruan languages (\citealp[114-115]{gualdieri1998}; \citealp[for an overview on Toba relative clauses, see][]{carpio.censabella2012,messineo.porta2009}). In (\ref{ex:det_noun_modifying}), for example, the nominal determiner \textit{so} introduces the extended one-place predicate. This clause modifies the P argument, which in turn expresses a locative role within the modifying clause.  

\begin{exe}
\ex  \label{ex:det_noun_modifying}
\gll eko s-oleːnta-ɢ-tak n-oʔɢoɾ-ik \textbf{so} s-atʃikqo-ta-ɡe  \\
{\Disc} {\First.\II}-remember-{\Pl}-{\Prog} {\Indet.\Poss}-be\_poor-{\Nmlz.\Obj.\Sg} {\DetTwo} {\First.\III}-descend.{\Pl}-{\Dur}-{\LocThree} \\	
\trans `...we are thinking about the poverty from where we are coming...'
\trans  \hfill(mocCA190924\_1: 00:02:08)
\end{exe}

Examples like (\ref{ex:det_noun_modifying}) illustrate the complexity involved in the expression of P arguments in natural speech. In these constructions, the same nominal is shared by the combined clauses. In the two-place predicate, predicate the P argument is only expressed lexically, whereas in the extended one-place predicate, the P argument is morphologically encoded, referring back to its co-nominal in the main clause.

\section{Agent foregrounding in the context of verb valence alternation}
After reviewing the morphosyntactic properties of argument expression, we now turn to the means by which P arguments can be demoted. We focus here on derivational elements \textit{-ɢan} and \textit{-taɢan}, which occur in positions 1 and 2 in \autoref{fig:verbstructure in Northern Chaco Mocoví.}. Such an derivational elements can increase and decrease verb valence, creating constructions that fall within the domains of causativization and antipassivization. In other words, these dedicated valence modifiers  primarily trigger changes in the morphosyntactic expression of S and A arguments, which naturally affects the status of the P argument.  The typologically unusual co-expression pattern has been studied by scholars working on Mocoví \citep{Carrio2015,Carrio2015a,Juarez2017c,juarez.alvarez-gonzalez2021} and is attested in other Guaycuruan languages as well. Cross-linguistically, only a  handful of languages -- not more than five languages, including Mocoví -- have been reported to display this specific valence co-expression pattern \citep[see][100-103]{bahrt2021}\footnote{However, this study fails to report several additional languages from the Gran Chaco region where this phenomenon is also attested}.  

When \textit{-ɢan} and \textit{-taɢan} function as intransitivizing devices, the resulting derived predicates are part of what I call agent foregrounding constructions. These stand in contrast to the subject defocusing construction, whose dedicated marker is \textit{qa-} and occurs in position -2.  Inspired by the framework on passives and related constructions in \citet{Shibatani1985a}, I use the notions of foregrounding and defocusing  as functional umbrella terms, indicating that subject and agent-like arguments either increase or decrease their discourse prominence. Functionally,  foregrounding and defocusing constructions mirror each other. In agent foregrounding constructions, specifically, the agent-like argument becomes structurally and discursively highly prominent. A by product of such a change in argument prominence is the demotion of the P argument, which structurally manifests in different ways, as we will see below (see  \ref{sec:structural_feautres_p_dem}). An example of an agent foregrounding construction is given in (\ref{ex:objsupkill}). In this example, the first use of the predicate \textit{kill} inflects intransitively and the entailed P argument is syntactically omitted. The predication is only about agent's activity and does not specify a clear, bounded outcome.  

\begin{exe}
\ex \textit{-ɢan} agent foregrounding construction
	 \gll so pjoq i-taː-taʔ-pe-ɡe so ʎik so jale \textbf{ɾ}-lawat-\textbf{ɢan} \textbf{i}-lawat a-\textbf{so} \textbf{una}{\Sploan} \textbf{neketeɡeɢaj}   \\
	{\DetTwo} dog {\Third.\II}-sniff-{\Pl}-{\Prog}-{\LocTwo} {\DetTwo} track {\DetTwo} man {\Third.\II.\Intr}-kill-{\Vm:\Intr}   {\Third.\II}-kill {\F}-{\DetTwo} {\Det} girl   \\
	\trans `The dog was sniffing the track of \textbf{the killer} (who) killed the girl.'
		\trans\hfill (mocCA130614\_1: 00:09:42)\label{ex:objsupkill}
\end{exe}

Furthermore, the agent foregrounding construction is both structurally and semantically connected to causative constructions, as illustrated in (\ref{ex:causefallb}). Morphologically, only a small class of one-place predicates can be causativized by \textit{-ɢan}, which introduces a cause participant, functioning as the A argument. This causativization process typically requires the bound person form \textit{i-}, which indexes third-person, transitive subjects. Semantically, both the agent foregrounding and the causative constructions involve an agent-like argument that functions either as S or A \citep[see][Ch.4]{juarez2023}   

\begin{exe}
\ex \textit{-ɢan} causative construction
\gll so jaɢat \textbf{i}-ʔɡen-\textbf{ɢan}-ɲi ɲi iːmek\\
{\DetTwo} rain  {\Third.\II}-fall-{\Vm:\Tr}-{\Dir:\Down} {\DetSix} house\\
\trans `The rain knocked down the house.'
\hfill{ (mocCA160720: 00:03:39)} \label{ex:causefallb}
\end{exe}


On the other hand, agent foregrounding constructions functionally contrast with the subject-defocusing construction, illustrated in \ref{ex:defckill}. In this construction, the subject is expressed as a non-referential, impersonal-like participant (e.g., someone, they, people) or as a  plural participant,  and only the P argument is discursively prominent. This clause does not inflect intransitively and its reading(s) is context dependent, always entailing a non-specific subject argument.

\begin{exe}
\ex Subject defocusing
	 \gll dʲoː dʒi i-wa \textbf{qa}-i-lawat \textbf{qa}-i-lawat dʒi i-wa \\
			       {\Exclam} {\DetFive} {\First\Sg.\Poss}-wife {\Sbj.\Defc}-{\Third.\II}-kill {\Sbj.\Defc}-{\Third.\II}-kill {\DetFive} {\First\Sg.\Poss}-wife\\
	\trans `...oh, someone killed \textbf{my wife}, someone  killed \textbf{my wife}...' \trans\hfill (mocCA191127\_2: 00:13:32)\label{ex:defckill}
\end{exe}


\subsection{Structural correlates and effects on the P argument}\label{sec:structural_feautres_p_dem}
To understand the morphological and syntactic nuances of agent foregrounding constructions, we begin by examining examples from controlled, elicited sentences. One of the salient features of Mocoví is its person-based alignment pattern, combining nominative-accusative and tripartite alignments. Thus, the structural correlates of the agent foregrounding constructions manifest slightly different whether the foregrounded A argument corresponds to a speech Act Participant or to a non-speech act participant. 

 First- and second-person arguments follow a nominative-accusative alignment at the indexing level, i.e., (A=S: s- $\neq$ P: ∅). Because of this, the foregrounding effect on the subject argument does not correlate with any formal change in its verbal expression. Foregrounding the A/S activity only leads to  disregarding the nominal determiner, as in (\ref{ex:kill_firstperson_a}), therefore focusing on the verb action itself rather than on its outcome. Consequently, the meaning of the verb also differs slightly from the base, unmarked transitive construction. The speaker entailment in (\ref{ex:kill_firstperson_b}), is that the agent has not killed  a pig yet, but he is about to go somewhere else to engage in that specific activity, without knowing whether the number of killed animals will be one or several.  


\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:kill_firstperson}
\begin{xlist}
\ex

As mentioned above, when two‑place predicates are marked by \textit{-ɢan} and the A argument corresponds to a non-speech act participant, these predicates usually call for the bound person \textit{ɾ-}, which indicates an intransitive subject. In addition, the P argument can be fully omitted (\ref{ex:intrtieupb}) or expressed as a bear noun without a nominal determiner (\ref{ex:intrtieupc}).  In both cases, the demoted P argument is interpreted as generic, lacking a sense of uniqueness, although subtle semantic differences remain. In these examples, the complete omission of the P argument allows for a much wider set of entities that could  potentially be tied up, e.g., horses, cows, sheep, and the like, and only contextual clues can help identify the specific entities involved in this event. By contrast, the use of a bare noun narrows the possible referents to a specific class, in this case \textit{horses}.

\subsection{How to recover the expression of P}
Although agent foregrounding construction indirectly involve P demotion, the language displays several morphosyntactic strategies to overtly reintroduce the demoted argument and narrow its referentiality in the immediate discourse context. These syntactic strategies respond to the functional need of avoiding ambiguity in participant referentiality as the discourse unfolds, specifically regarding the erstwhile demoted P argument.


One of the strategies to encode the P argument of a detransitivized predicate is by the juxtaposition of two predicates that otherwise  function as individual verbs on their own. This combination of predicates produces structures similar in many regards to serial verb constructions \citep[see][]{Aikhenvald2006, Bisang2009, Haspelmath2016}, although I do not treat them as a single complex clause here.  The multiverbal strategy to encode the P argument of a detransitivized predicate can be further divided according to the valence of the combined predicates. On the one hand, we find combined predicates like \textit{ɾ-kin-ɢan} `She/He greets (somebody)' and \textit{i-kin} `She/He greets him/her' in (\ref{ex:objincl1}). This sequence of predicates involves then the \textit{ɢan-}intransitivized predicate combined with a basic two-place predicate, creating a clause with the following transitivity pattern: \textsc{intr} + \textsc{tr}.  
           
\begin{exe}
\ex 
	 \gll ∅-k-pe-ɡe-eʔ ɾ-kin-\textbf{ɢan} i-\textbf{kin} \textbf{dʒi} \textbf{l}-\textbf{jaːle}-\textbf{qa}-\textbf{pi}   \\
			        {\Third.\II}-move-pe-{\LocTwo}-{\Pl} {\Third.\II.\Intr}-greet-{\Vm:\Intr} {\Third.\II}-greet {\DetFive} {\Third.\Poss}-descendent-{\Pl}-{\Coll}\\
	\trans `S/he goes to greet his/her children.'
\trans	 Lit. S/He goes, (S/he) greets, (S/he) greets his/her children.
	\trans \hfill  (mocCA160725: 00:55:30)\label{ex:objincl1}
\end{exe}

 Note that the selection of the second predicate does not appear to be arbitrary, as it repeats the  same verb root, \textit{-kin} `greet', on which the \textit{ɢan-}intransitivized predicates is built. Thus, one could suggest that the greeting event depicted in the translation is made of two predications semantically connected by the shared verb meaning. While the first predication depicts the agentivity of the A argument, the second one helps to introduce the actual referent of the P argument. 

Another possibility to express the syntactically omitted P argument is illustrated in (\ref{ex:objincl2}). Unlike (\ref{ex:objincl1}), the first detransitivized predicate \textit{-kin-ɢan} is further derived by the stacking of \textit{-ɢan} in its transitivizing function, which adds a new subject argument to the verb valency. This creates a morphologically complex predicate that combines with another non-derived two-place predicate. Both conjoined predicates show the following transitivity pattern: \textsc{tr} + \textsc{tr}. Like (\ref{ex:objincl1}), the main function of the second predicate is the syntactic expression of the P argument that was part of the verb root employed in the double-derived predicate.  

\begin{exe}
\ex 
	 \gll ajim s-kin-\textbf{ɢan}-\textbf{ɢan} so i-jaːle-k i-\textbf{kin} \textbf{so} \textbf{l}-\textbf{piɾ}   \\
			        {\First\Sg.\Pron} {\First.II}-greet-{\Vm:\Intr}-{\Vm:\Tr} {\DetTwo} {\First\Sg.\Poss}-descendent-{\M} {\Third.\II}-greet {\DetTwo} {\Third.\Poss}-grandfather\\
	\trans `I made my son greet his grandfather.'
	\trans    \hfill(mocCA160725: 00:46:37)\label{ex:objincl2}
\end{exe}

 The set of examples above includes a derived verb root categorized as a two-place predicate in its basic non-derived use. The multiverb strategy to encode the omitted P argument is also possible if the verb root is of one-place. The main condition, however, is that the combined predicates adjust to one of the transitivity patterns already mentioned, specifically:  \textsc{intr} + \textsc{tr}. To achieve this syntactic pattern, the basic one-place predicate must be further derived.  First,  note that the \textit{taɢan-}intransitivized predicate does not accept the syntactic expression of P, as in (\ref{ex:showernoobj}). But, in order to include it, the addition of the transitivized predicate \textit{-tʃil-ɢan} `shower him/her' is needed (\ref{ex:showerwithobject}). 

\begin{exe}
\ex\label{ex:}
\begin{xlist}
\ex 	
\gll ɾ-tʃil-ɢan-taɢan (\Ungr so qaɾ-qaja-ɾ-pi)\\
     {\Third.\III}-shower-{\Vm:\Tr}-{\Vm:\Intr} {}{\DetTwo} {\First\Pl.\Poss}-brother-{\Pl}-{\Coll}  \\
\trans `S/He baptizes (someone).'
\trans  \hfill  (mocCA160726: 00:56:25)\label{ex:showernoobj}
	
\ex 
\gll \textbf{ɾ}-atʃel-ɢan-\textbf{taɢan}-tak n-atʃil-ɢan-tak so qaɾ-qaja-ɾ-pi i-noɢon-o ke-ɾa n-qaːt-ek  \\
{\Third.\III.\Intr}-shower-{\Vm:\Tr}-{\Vm:\Intr}-{\Prog} {\Third.\III}-shower-{\Vm:\Tr}-{\Prog} {\DetTwo} {\First\Pl.\Poss}-brother-{\Pl}-{\Coll} {\Third.\II}-enter-{\Dir:\In} {\Obl}-{\DetFour} {\Indet.\Poss}-speak-{\Sg.\Obj.\Nmlz}\\
\trans `He is baptizing our brothers who joined the Word (of God).'
\trans Lit. `He is baptizing, (he) is baptizing our brothers, (they) went into the Word.'
\trans \hfill  (mocCA160726: 00:46:37) \label{ex:showerwithobject}	
\end{xlist}

In the combined predicates like (\ref{ex:objincl1}), (\ref{ex:objincl2}), and (\ref{ex:showerwithobject}) the second predicate introducing the P argument appears to be lexically restricted to the same verb root that serves as the base for the \textit{ɢan-} or \textit{taɢan-} detransitivized predicates. 

Finally, another way to encode the former P argument of the detransitivized predicates is by marking it as an oblique, as illustrated in (\ref{ex:objincl3}). This strategy is possible once the verb valence of the base predicate is re-augmented by \textit{-ɢan}, which results in the double-derived verb \textit{-kin-ɢan-ɢan} `made to greet'. Rather than resulting in a clause with two non-subject arguments, this type of double derived predicate includes only two core arguments, the A and the P, in addition to the oblique-marked participant. 

\begin{exe}
\ex 
	 \gll so i-taʔa ajim i-kin-ɢan-ɢan \textbf{ke}-so qaɾ-piɾ     \\
			 {\DetTwo} {\First\Sg.\Poss}-father {\First\Sg.\Pron} {\Third\II}-greet-{\Vm:\Intr}-{\Vm:\Tr} {\Obl}-{\DetTwo} {\First\Pl.\Poss}-grandfather\\
	\trans `My dad made me greet our grandfather.'
	\trans `Mi papá me hizo saludar a nuestro abuelo.’ \hfill (mocCA160725: 01:01:22) \label{ex:objincl3}
\end{exe}
 
It must be mentioned, however, that examples like (\ref{ex:objincl3}), are rather infrequent in natural discourse, but naturally accepted when constructed via elicitation techniques. A much larger corpus-based study should elucidate the discourse factors playing a role in the selection of either the oblique-marked expression of the demoted P argument or the multi-verbal strategy, with the demoted P as a core argument of the doubled verb root. This task is beyond the scope of this paper. 

\section{Lexical distribution and importance for lexicon building}\label{sec:lexicon_distribution}
Since agent foregrounding constructions overlap in many respects with properties associated with antipassive constructions, I draw on recent typological observations on antipassives to account for the distributional properties of the agent foregrounding morphological markers. \autoref{tab:predicates taking -ɢan and -taɢan} summarizes the distribution of the two‑place predicates that occur with \textit{‑ɢan} and \textit{‑taɢan}.

First, note that these elements exhibit complementary and asymmetric distributional patterns. Specifically, predicates that are detransitivized by \textit{‑ɢan} do not occur with \textit{‑taɢan}, and vice versa. Additionally, the shorter form shows a much wider distribution than the longer form, which is restricted to only a handful of predicates. Furthermore, although previous studies have not explicitly addressed the frequency of these suffixes, examples involving \textit{‑ɢan} outnumber those with \textit{‑taɢan} in the available linguistic descriptions of Mocoví \citep[see][]{gualdieri1998,Carrio2015,Carrio2015a}. Such an asymmetric distributional pattern is not unusual in languages that possess multiple antipassive marker \citep[see, for instance,][]{say2021,janic.witzlack-makarevich2021}. \citep[][]{serzant.etal2021}, furthermore, argue that frequency plays a role in the asymmetric distribution of antipassive markers coexisting in the same language. Specifically, they propose that ``the most frequent lexical input will correlate with the less marking on the verb and viceversa".

  Second, as far as I am aware the only  lexical constraint conditioning which type of predicate can fall within agent foregrounding constructions has to do with the basic verb valence. From the verb list above and natural discourse data, it becomes clear that the  relevant parameter to express these types of constructions is that the base predicate corresponds to a two-place base. In fact, the employment of \textit{-ɢan} and \textit{-taɢan} as detransitivizers is a natural language-specific morphological test to identify the class of two-place predicates. Regardless of the basic verb valence, it is worth noticing nevertheless that predicates in \autoref{tab:predicates taking -ɢan and -taɢan} align to some of the properties of what \cite[see][182]{say2021} defines as the core of natural antipassives and the verb types that typically allow antipassivization \citep[see, the verb hierarchy proposed by][]{malchukov2015}. Semantically, predicates in their basic form cover a large range of verb classes, including not not only proper activities (`greet'), but also contact (`hit'), pursuit (`search'), cognition (`know', `remember') and speaking verbs (`ask', `ask for'), among others.

   Typologically, it is not unusual that ingestive verbs show idiosyncratic behaviours with respect to valence alternation patterns that they can participate and the transitivity profile of verb lexicon \citep[see][]{Nichols2004}. In a typological survey conducted by \citet[][63-65]{Dixon2000} on causative constructions, he concluded that ``drinking and eating are the transitive activities which people are most likely to make other people do, on every other continent". Similarly, for those languages having an intransitive constraint on morphological causativization, \citet[][30-31]{Naess2009} and \citet[][48-54]{Amberber2009} observed more recently that not only are `eat' and `drink' flexible in terms of causativization but also ``other verbs referring to acts of  ingestion or consumption'', e.g.  `smoke' and `lick', as well as verbs of perception, e.g. `know'.  
   
   Lastly, given that the main focus of agent foregrounding constructions is on the agent's activity, it naturally follows that animacy of the demoted P argument is an irrelevant parameter of variation. Typological work has noted that the animacy of the demoted P argument plays a role in the variation that antipassives display \citep{say2021,zuniga.kittila2019}. Again, the list of predicates in \autoref{tab:predicates taking -ɢan and -taɢan} and the examples discussed so far show that both animate and inanimate can be part of implied demoted P argument, although  the implication of either one of these participants can be semantically much more appropriate to different predicates. For example, an animate P argument is implied with predicates such as \textit{-kin} `greet' or \textit{-laɾ} `send/order', but an inanimate participant is logically involved with predicates such as \textit{-qawalat} `knead' or \textit{-peloɡ} `rake'. Yet, there are other predicates that can include both types of P arguments depending on the discourse context, e.g., \textit{-tʃaq} `cut' or \textit{-lat} `leave'. \section{Discourse distribution}{\label{sec:annotation-scheema}}
One of the facts that has not received much attention with respect to the use agent foregrounding constructions has to do with their discourse distribution. Thus, this section explores the actual discourse occurrence of \textit{-ɢan} and \textit{-taɢan} in natural speech. The goal is purely descriptive to understand the basic questions as to how frequent the types of agent foregrounding predicates are in natural discourse and likewise what correspondence between types of predicates and the expression of the P arguments can be found  \citep[for a similar example, see][]{haude.allassonniere-tang2023}. Although the nature of these questions is quite basic, such a quantitative task has not been carried out to study certain valence-changing operations in Mocoví nor other Guaycuruan languages. There are, however, other relevant prior works dealing with, for example, counts of lexical argument expression in Mocoví \citep[see][]{califa2014} and Chaco Toba's discourse \citep[see][]{cuneo.messineo2019}.

The case study presented here builds on a single narrative discourse as a first approach to complement the qualitative observations on the distribution and function of agent foregrounding constructions. The narrative has discourse characteristics of a monologue, where the speaker talks about different past and present life events. Building on clause types and argument expression presented in \ref{sec:clause-types}, clauses are annotated according to following parameters: (i) predicate meaning, (ii) valence type, (iii)  P argument expression and (iv) type of expression of the P argument. There is an additional parameter that is only valid to P argument of third person, which considers the type of determiner present in the expression of nominal P arguments.
