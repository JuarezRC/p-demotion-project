\begin{abstract}
This paper examines agent foregrounding constructions in Northern Chaco Mocov√≠ and their effects on the expression of P arguments. Building on a description of clause types and argument expression, the analysis focuses on the derivational markers \textit{‚Äë…¢an} and \textit{‚Äëta…¢an}, which combine detransitivizing and causativizing functions. When used as intransitivizers, these markers increase the prominence of the agent and demote the P argument, giving rise to a set of constructions functionally comparable to antipassives. Based on both elicited and natural discourse data, the study identifies the morphosyntactic correlates of P‚Äëargument demotion, the conditions under which P arguments may or may not be expressed, and the discourse strategies used to reintroduce them. The paper also shows that the detransitivized forms play a central role in lexicon building, especially in the formation of derived nouns. A quantitative case study complements the qualitative analysis by illustrating the distribution of agent foregrounding  and morphologically related constructions in natural speech. The findings contribute to understanding valence alternation, argument expression, and lexical derivation in Mocov√≠ and to broader typological discussions on antipassive‚Äërelated phenomena.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textit{Keywords}: agent foregrounding, P-demotion, Northern Chaco Mocov√≠, lexical distribution, frequency 

\section{Introduction}

This paper examines the morphosyntactic and discourse properties of agent foregrounding constructions in Northern Chaco Mocov√≠. These constructions arise through the use of the derivational markers \textit{‚Äë…¢an} and \textit{‚Äëta…¢an}, which participate in a broader system of valence‚Äëchanging morphology. When used as detransitivizers, these markers reduce the verbal valence of two‚Äëplace predicates and highlight the agent‚Äôs activity, contributing in the demotion or omission of the P argument. Because this function overlaps with properties commonly associated with antipassive constructions, the analysis also draws on recent typological work on antipassives to contextualize the Mocov√≠ patterns.

The study first describes the relevant clause types and the mechanisms by which Mocov√≠ expresses core arguments. Particular attention is given to the role of nominal determiners, the interaction between verb morphology and argument structure, and the conditions that license or block the expression of the P argument. The paper then explores how the detransitivizing use of \textit{‚Äë…¢an} and \textit{‚Äëta…¢an} affects the presence, form, and interpretation of the P argument, especially in relation to definiteness, referentiality, and argument indexing.

Based on elicited data and natural discourse, the analysis identifies several strategies for recovering or reintroducing the P argument after demotion, including multiverbal sequences and oblique‚Äëmarked participants. The distribution of these strategies is examined in relation to verb valence, predicate semantics, and narrative context. Additionally, the paper shows that detransitivized predicates play an important role in lexicon building, serving as bases for derived nouns formed through the nominalizer \textit{‚Äë…¢ak}.

A small case‚Äëstudy based on a narrative text provides a first quantitative perspective on the frequency and discourse behavior of these constructions. Although agent foregrounding predicates occur relatively infrequently in discourse, their distribution is systematic and reflects functional motivations related to participant tracking and event structure. Overall, the study contributes to understanding how Mocov√≠ organizes valence alternation, argument prominence, and lexical derivation within its verbal system.

\section{The Mocov√≠ people and their language}
Mocov√≠ is part of the Guaycuruan family along with other four languages, three of which are still spoken today, i.e., Kadiw√©o, Toba and Pilag√°. Mocov√≠ communities are located in northeastern Argentina, as shown in Map \ref{fig:moc-communities}\footnote{Personal elaboration based on the name of Mocov√≠ communities as reported in different studies on Mocov√≠ language and culture; \cite[see, among others,][]{Altman2010,Altman2011,carrio2009,Dalla-CorteCaballero2012,gualdieri1998,grondona1998,iparraguirre2011,Iparraguirre2016,Lopez2017}.}. Demographically, as one moves further north within the chain of Mocov√≠ communities, it becomes increasingly common  to find families that have been in contact with Toba-speaking communities for over a century or more. The specific social and linguistic outcomes of this long-term contact situation are still in need of further research.

 Following a traditional view on clause classification, here I define types of clauses by the valence profile of their predicates  \citep{Dryer2007,Mithun2005,Mithun2005a} and  their (morpho)syntactic patterning. This latter feature is in turn recognized by the way in which arguments are treated with respect to verb indexing, word order and oblique flagging. \citep[see][3-4]{comrie.etal2015}. The (morpho-)syntactic pattern of a clause will then serve to recognize clause types such as intransitive and transitive clauses, among other language-specific subtypes. From a typological angle, it is often mentioned that certain classes of predicates are taken to be present in almost all languages, namely mono-valent and bi-valent predicates, whereas  other types of predicates are subject to more language-specific variation, e.g. avalent predicates \citep[][]{Malchukov2010}. Mocov√≠, and to a large extent its Guaycuruan sister languages, is distinctive in that predicates are lexically categorized as either mono-valent or bi-valent and that these predicate fall into different morphosyntactic patterns. In what follows the major clause types relevant for this study are described.  

Let us begin with typical one-place predicates. Examples of this type of predicate are \textit{-o îon} `get married' or \textit{-inta} `be similar' in (\ref{ex:clause_intrans}). In predicates of this class. the main argument expression is the bound person form, which encodes the  single argument (S). Co-nominals or pronominal elements of S arguments tend to be overt only for referent identification. Less relevant for this paper are verbless or non-verbal clauses like \textit{jim …¢o…æatom}, where the juxtaposition of elements suffice for a predication.  

\begin{exe}
 
 \ex One-place predicate clause   
  \gll \textbf{s}-\textbf{o îon} qam qajka ka \textbf{s}-\textbf{inta} jim …¢o…æa-ta-om \\
 {\First.\I}-get\_married but {\Exist.\Neg} {\DetOne} {\First.\II}-be\_similar {\First.\Sg.\Pron} be\_poor-{\Dur}-{\Ints} \\
 \trans ‚Äò... I got married, but I was not the same, I was very poor...' \hfill (mocCA190924\_1: 00:02:20) \label{ex:clause_intrans}
\end{exe}
 
 A slightly different type of clause includes the extended one-place predicate, as in (\ref{ex:extended-oneplace}). This clause takes a one-place predicate as its base but the number of arguments is extended by a locative-orientative suffix (which occur in position 8, see \autoref{fig:verbstructure in Northern Chaco Mocov√≠.}). In this case, the suffix \textit{-…°e} entails the locative participant as a core argument of the clause, while keeping the morphological intransitive expression of the S argument. As noted in this example, the extended P argument is expressed morphologically and lexically. Clauses of this type have received a great deal of attention in Guaycuruan languages  and the function of spatial morphology in contexts like these brought novel data to the typologically unusual link between the domain of spatial morphology and valence increasing.

In the second case, similar to one-place predicates, two-place predicates can also occur with locative-orientative morphology or other suffixes encoding P arguments. The effect that locative-directional suffixes have on two-place predicates varies, as these suffixes can simply provide spatial information regarding  the P argument or actually extend the number of arguments. Other suffixes expressing a P argument merely serve to indicate the presence of such argument, indexing non-singular number of the P argument. In (\ref{ex:extended_twoplace_a}), P arguments are indexed by spatial and non-spatial morphology. Here the speaker narrates about his past hunting experience and what kind of animals he used to hunt. On the other hand, the sequence \textit{a-lo} simply expresses the multiplicity of entities denoted by the P nominal. This is so regardless of the referential nouns may or may not include plural marking morphology.

 Natural discourse examples presented in this section show that while the S and A arguments are invariably indexed in the verb complex, the overt realization of a P argument can be either only nominal or pronominal, or can be co-expressed by verbal morphology and its corresponding nominal or pronominal expression. This argument expression variability is relevant for the data annotation and is implemented for capturing the range of possibilities for expressing P argument in discourse.  
 
\subsection{The expression of P arguments: nominal determiners and their uses}

The closed class of six nominal determiners encodes different semantic values of the nominal referent, showing a clustering of salient typological features \citep[cf.][178-181]{Aikhenvald2000}. These semantic features include presence vs. absence, position (lying, standing or sitting) and movement/proximity (coming to the speaker or away from the speaker).

In addition to the semantic features mentioned above, nominal determiners contribute to the definiteness of nominal referents. In this regards, defined and highly identifiable nouns follow  nominal determiners, as in (\ref{ex:determiner_p_ka}). Furthermore, the selection of determiners does not seem to obey exclusively to the semantic properties of the nominal referent, as discourse factors also appear to play a role. Note in the example below, for example, that the selection of the nominal determiner could have been \textit{…≤i}, as referents of this kind are commonly conceived in a sitting position. Instead, the speaker selected \textit{ka}, which encodes absence of the referent. This nominal determiner fits the discourse context, however, as the speaker narrates events from a remote past, i.e., childhood, which naturally implies that the nominal referent is not present at the moment of speech.   

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:determiner_p_ka}
\gll sa-s-awan-qa-pe-ge-a \textbf{ka} \textbf{mala î} \\
{\Neg}-{\First.\II}-see-{\Pl}-{\Prog}-{\LocThree}-{\Obj.\Arg} {\DetOne} bed \\
\trans `...we didn't know the bed...' \hfill(mocCA191122\_2: 00:01:48) 
\end{exe}

However, depending on the referent and, to some extent on the predicate type, nominal determiners can be omitted, which also affects the referential properties of the noun. Example (\ref{ex:no_determiner}) shows that the noun \textit{toÀê…æta} occurs without a nominal determiner in the eating event. The reference here is not to a single entity, but to multiple entities, which is reinforced by the dinamicity of the eating event in its progressive form. Change in the referentiality of determined and undetermined nouns is more obvious if we compare the expression of A and P arguments. Whereas the former occurs with the determiner \textit{so} and the referent is a single, individualized entity, the latter lacks a determiner and its referentiality is less individualized.

Lastly, it is worth mentioning that nominal determiners have a broader range of uses and constitute one of the devices available in the language for discourse linking. Of particular relevance for this work is their clause-combining function, where they  introduce  a noun-modifying clause similar to relative clauses. This property of nominal determiner is not exclusive to Mocov√≠, as it has also been reported for other Guaycuruan languages (\citealp[114-115]{gualdieri1998}; \citealp[for an overview on Toba relative clauses, see][]{carpio.censabella2012,messineo.porta2009}). In (\ref{ex:det_noun_modifying}), for example, the nominal determiner \textit{so} introduces the extended one-place predicate. This clause modifies the P argument, which in turn expresses a locative role within the modifying clause (see also  \ref{ex:p-expression-indef} where a nominal determiner introduces an object complement-like clause).  

\begin{exe}

\section{Agent foregrounding in the context of valence alternations}
After reviewing the morphosyntactic properties of argument expression, we now turn to the means by which P arguments can be demoted. We focus here on derivational elements \textit{-…¢an} and \textit{-ta…¢an}, which occur in positions 1 and 2 in \autoref{fig:verbstructure in Northern Chaco Mocov√≠.}. Such an derivational elements can increase and decrease verb valence, creating constructions that fall within the domains of causativization and antipassivization. In other words, these dedicated valence modifiers  primarily trigger changes in the morphosyntactic expression of S and A arguments, which naturally affects semantic and morphosyntactic properties of P arguments.  The typologically unusual co-expression pattern has been studied by scholars working on Mocov√≠ \citep{Carrio2015,Carrio2015a,Juarez2017c,juarez.alvarez-gonzalez2021} and is attested in other Guaycuruan languages as well. Cross-linguistically, only a  handful of languages -- not more than five languages, including Mocov√≠ -- have been reported to display this specific valence co-expression pattern \citep[see][100-103]{bahrt2021}\footnote{However, this study fails to report several additional languages from the Gran Chaco region where this phenomenon is also attested}.  

When \textit{-…¢an} and \textit{-ta…¢an} function as intransitivizing devices, the resulting derived predicates are part of what I call agent foregrounding constructions. These stand in contrast to the subject defocusing construction, whose dedicated marker is \textit{qa-} and occurs in position -2.  Inspired by the framework on passives and related constructions in \citet{Shibatani1985a}, I use the notions of foregrounding and defocusing  as functional umbrella terms, indicating that subject and agent-like arguments either increase or decrease their discourse prominence. Functionally,  foregrounding and defocusing constructions mirror each other with respect to the effect that they have on the subject argument of these constructions.  

xIn agent foregrounding constructions, specifically, the agent-like argument becomes structurally and discursively highly prominent. A by product of such a change in argument prominence is the demotion of the P argument, which structurally manifests in different ways, as we will see below (see  \ref{sec:structural_feautres_p_dem}). An example of an agent foregrounding construction is given in (\ref{ex:objsupkill}). In this example, the first use of the predicate \textit{kill} inflects intransitively and the entailed P argument is syntactically omitted. The predication is only about the agent's activity and does not specify a clear, bounded outcome.  

\begin{exe}
\ex \textit{-…¢an} agent foregrounding construction
	 \gll so pjoq i-taÀê-ta î-pe-…°e so  éik so jale \textbf{…æ}-lawat-\textbf{…¢an} \textbf{i}-lawat a-\textbf{so} \textbf{una}{\Sploan} \textbf{nekete…°e…¢aj}   \\
	{\DetTwo} dog {\Third.\II}-sniff-{\Pl}-{\Prog}-{\LocTwo} {\DetTwo} track {\DetTwo} man {\Third.\II.\Intr}-kill-{\Vm:\Intr}   {\Third.\II}-kill {\F}-{\DetTwo} {\Det} girl   \\
	\trans `The dog was sniffing the track of \textbf{the killer} (who) killed the girl.'
		\trans\hfill (mocCA130614\_1: 00:09:42)\label{ex:objsupkill}
\end{exe}

Furthermore, the agent foregrounding construction is both structurally and semantically connected to causative constructions, as illustrated in (\ref{ex:causefallb}). Morphologically, only a small class of one-place predicates can be causativized by \textit{-…¢an}, which introduces a cause participant, functioning as the A argument. This causativization process typically requires the bound person form \textit{i-}, which indexes third-person, transitive subjects. Semantically, both the agent foregrounding and the causative constructions involve an agent-like argument that functions either as S (in the detransitivized clause) or A (in the causative clause) \citep[see][Ch.4]{juarez2023}   

\begin{exe}
\ex \textit{-…¢an} causative construction
\gll so ja…¢at \textbf{i}- î…°en-\textbf{…¢an}-…≤i …≤i iÀêmek\\
{\DetTwo} rain  {\Third.\II}-fall-{\Vm:\Tr}-{\Dir:\Down} {\DetSix} house\\
\trans `The rain knocked down the house.'
\hfill{ (mocCA160720: 00:03:39)} \label{ex:causefallb}
\end{exe}


On the other hand, agent foregrounding constructions functionally contrast with the subject-defocusing construction, illustrated in (\ref{ex:defckill}). In this construction, the subject is expressed as a non-referential, impersonal-like participant (e.g., someone, they, people) or as a  plural participant,  and only the P argument is discursively prominent. This clause does not inflect intransitively and its reading(s) is context dependent, always entailing a non-specific subject argument.

\begin{exe}
\ex Subject defocusing
	 \gll d ≤oÀê d íi i-wa \textbf{qa}-i-lawat \textbf{qa}-i-lawat d íi i-wa \\
			       {\Exclam} {\DetFive} {\First\Sg.\Poss}-wife {\Sbj.\Defc}-{\Third.\II}-kill {\Sbj.\Defc}-{\Third.\II}-kill {\DetFive} {\First\Sg.\Poss}-wife\\
	\trans `...oh, someone killed \textbf{my wife}, someone  killed \textbf{my wife}...' \trans\hfill (mocCA191127\_2: 00:13:32)\label{ex:defckill}
\end{exe}


\subsection{Structural correlates and effects on the P argument}\label{sec:structural_feautres_p_dem}
To understand the morphological and syntactic nuances of agent foregrounding constructions, we begin by examining examples from controlled, elicited sentences. One of the salient features of Mocov√≠ is its person-based alignment pattern, combining nominative-accusative and tripartite alignments. Thus, the structural correlates of the agent foregrounding constructions manifest slightly different whether the foregrounded A argument corresponds to a speech act participant or to a non-speech act participant. 

 First- and second-person arguments follow a nominative-accusative alignment at the indexing level -- consider, for instance, A=S: s- $\neq$ P: ‚àÖ. Because of this alignment type, the foregrounding effect on the subject argument does not correlate with any formal change in its verbal expression. Foregrounding the A/S activity only leads to  disregarding the nominal determiner, as in (\ref{ex:kill_firstperson_a}), therefore focusing on the verb action itself rather than on its outcome. Consequently, the meaning of the verb also differs slightly from the base, unmarked transitive construction. The entailment in (\ref{ex:kill_firstperson_b}), is that the agent has not killed  a pig yet, but he is about to go somewhere else to engage in that specific activity, without knowing whether the number of killed animals will be one or several.  


\begin{exe}

\ex

	{\First\Sg.\Pron} {\First.\II}-kill-{\Vm:\Intr}  pig \\
	\trans `I'm going to slaughter (a) pig(s).' \label{ex:kill_firstperson_b} \hfill(mocCA160725: 00:35:22)

 \end{xlist}
\end{exe}

As mentioned above, when two‚Äëplace predicates are marked by \textit{-…¢an} and the A argument corresponds to a non-speech act participant, these predicates usually call for the bound person \textit{…æ-}, which indicates an intransitive subject. In addition, the P argument can be fully omitted (\ref{ex:intrtieupb}) or expressed as a bear noun without a nominal determiner (\ref{ex:intrtieupc}).  In both cases, the demoted P argument is interpreted as generic, lacking a sense of uniqueness, although subtle semantic differences remain. In these examples, the complete omission of the P argument allows for a much wider set of entities that could  potentially be tied up, e.g., horses, cows, sheep, and the like, and only contextual clues can help identify the specific entities involved in this event. By contrast, the use of a bare noun narrows the possible referents to a specific class, in this case \textit{horses}.

\subsection{How to recover the expression of P}
Although agent foregrounding construction indirectly involve P demotion, the language displays several morphosyntactic strategies to overtly reintroduce the demoted argument and narrow its referentiality in the immediate discourse context. These syntactic strategies respond to the functional need of avoiding ambiguity in participant referentiality as the discourse unfolds, specifically regarding the erstwhile demoted P argument.


One of the strategies to encode the P argument of a detransitivized predicate is by the juxtaposition of two predicates that otherwise  function as individual verbs on their own. This combination of predicates produces structures similar in many regards to serial verb constructions \citep[see][]{Aikhenvald2006, Bisang2009, Haspelmath2016}, although I do not treat them as a single complex clause here.  The multiverbal strategy to encode the P argument of a detransitivized predicate can be further divided according to the valence of the combined predicates. On the one hand, we find combined predicates like \textit{…æ-kin-…¢an} `She/He greets (somebody)' and \textit{i-kin} `She/He greets him/her' in (\ref{ex:objincl1}). This sequence of predicates involves then the \textit{…¢an-}intransitivized predicate combined with a basic two-place predicate, creating a clause with the following transitivity pattern: \textsc{intr} + \textsc{tr}.  
           
\begin{exe}
\ex 
	 \gll ‚àÖ-k-pe-…°e-e î …æ-kin-\textbf{…¢an} i-\textbf{kin} \textbf{d íi} \textbf{l}-\textbf{jaÀêle}-\textbf{qa}-\textbf{pi}   \\
			        {\Third.\II}-move-pe-{\LocTwo}-{\Pl} {\Third.\II.\Intr}-greet-{\Vm:\Intr} {\Third.\II}-greet {\DetFive} {\Third.\Poss}-descendent-{\Pl}-{\Coll}\\
	\trans `S/he goes to greet his/her children.'
\trans	 Lit. S/He goes, (S/he) greets, (S/he) greets his/her children.
	\trans \hfill  (mocCA160725: 00:55:30)\label{ex:objincl1}
\end{exe}

 Note that the selection of the second predicate does not appear to be arbitrary, as it repeats the  same verb root, \textit{-kin} `greet', on which the \textit{…¢an-}intransitivized predicates is built. Thus, one could suggest that the greeting event depicted in the translation is made of two predications semantically connected by the shared verb meaning. While the first predication depicts the agentivity of the A argument, the second one helps to introduce the actual referent of the previously demoted P argument. 

Another possibility to express the syntactically omitted P argument is illustrated in (\ref{ex:objincl2}). Unlike (\ref{ex:objincl1}), the first detransitivized predicate \textit{-kin-…¢an} is further derived by the stacking of \textit{-…¢an} in its transitivizing function, which adds a new subject argument to the verb valency. This creates a morphologically complex predicate that combines with another non-derived two-place predicate. Both conjoined predicates show the following transitivity pattern: \textsc{tr} + \textsc{tr}. Like (\ref{ex:objincl1}), the main function of the second predicate is the syntactic expression of the P argument that was part of the verb root employed in the double-derived predicate.  

\begin{exe}
\ex 
	 \gll ajim s-kin-\textbf{…¢an}-\textbf{…¢an} so i-jaÀêle-k i-\textbf{kin} \textbf{so} \textbf{l}-\textbf{pi…æ}   \\
			        {\First\Sg.\Pron} {\First.II}-greet-{\Vm:\Intr}-{\Vm:\Tr} {\DetTwo} {\First\Sg.\Poss}-descendent-{\M} {\Third.\II}-greet {\DetTwo} {\Third.\Poss}-grandfather\\
	\trans `I made my son greet his grandfather.'  \hfill(mocCA160725: 00:46:37)\label{ex:objincl2}
\end{exe}

 The set of examples above includes a derived verb root categorized as a two-place predicate in its basic non-derived use. The multiverb strategy to encode the omitted P argument is also possible if the verb root is of one-place. The main condition, however, is that the combined predicates adjust to one of the transitivity patterns already mentioned, specifically:  \textsc{intr} + \textsc{tr}. To achieve this syntactic pattern, the basic one-place predicate must be further derived.  First,  note that the \textit{ta…¢an-}intransitivized predicate does not accept the syntactic expression of P, as in (\ref{ex:showernoobj}). But, in order to include it, the addition of the transitivized predicate \textit{-t Éil-…¢an} `shower him/her' is needed (\ref{ex:showerwithobject}). 

\begin{exe}
\ex\label{ex:}
\begin{xlist}
\ex 	
\gll …æ-t Éil-…¢an-ta…¢an (\Ungr so qa…æ-qaja-…æ-pi)\\
     {\Third.\III}-shower-{\Vm:\Tr}-{\Vm:\Intr} {}{\DetTwo} {\First\Pl.\Poss}-brother-{\Pl}-{\Coll}  \\
\trans `S/He baptizes (someone).'  \hfill  (mocCA160726: 00:56:25)\label{ex:showernoobj}
	
\ex 
\gll \textbf{…æ}-at Éel-…¢an-\textbf{ta…¢an}-tak n-at Éil-…¢an-tak so qa…æ-qaja-…æ-pi i-no…¢on-o ke-…æa n-qaÀêt-ek  \\
{\Third.\III.\Intr}-shower-{\Vm:\Tr}-{\Vm:\Intr}-{\Prog} {\Third.\III}-shower-{\Vm:\Tr}-{\Prog} {\DetTwo} {\First\Pl.\Poss}-brother-{\Pl}-{\Coll} {\Third.\II}-enter-{\Dir:\In} {\Obl}-{\DetFour} {\Indet.\Poss}-speak-{\Sg.\Obj.\Nmlz}\\
\trans `He is baptizing our brothers who joined the Word (of God).'
\trans Lit. `He is baptizing, (he) is baptizing our brothers, (they) went into the Word.'
\trans \hfill  (mocCA160726: 00:46:37) \label{ex:showerwithobject}

In the combined predicates like (\ref{ex:objincl1}), (\ref{ex:objincl2}), and (\ref{ex:showerwithobject}) the second predicate introducing the P argument appears to be lexically restricted to the same verb root that serves as the base for the \textit{…¢an-} or \textit{ta…¢an-} detransitivized predicates. 

Finally, another way to encode the former P argument of the detransitivized predicates is by marking it as an oblique, as illustrated in (\ref{ex:objincl3}). This strategy is possible once the verb valence of the base predicate is re-augmented by \textit{-…¢an}, which results in the double-derived verb \textit{-kin-…¢an-…¢an} `made to greet'. Rather than resulting in a clause with two non-subject arguments, this type of double derived predicate includes only two core arguments, the A and the P, in addition to the oblique-marked participant. 

\begin{exe}
\ex 
	 \gll so i-ta îa ajim i-kin-…¢an-…¢an \textbf{ke}-so qa…æ-pi…æ     \\
			 {\DetTwo} {\First\Sg.\Poss}-father {\First\Sg.\Pron} {\Third\II}-greet-{\Vm:\Intr}-{\Vm:\Tr} {\Obl}-{\DetTwo} {\First\Pl.\Poss}-grandfather\\
	\trans `My dad made me greet our grandfather.' \hfill (mocCA160725: 01:01:22) \label{ex:objincl3}
\end{exe}
 
It must be mentioned, however, that examples like (\ref{ex:objincl3}), are rather infrequent in natural discourse, but naturally accepted when constructed via elicitation techniques. A much larger corpus-based study should elucidate the discourse factors playing a role in the selection of either the oblique-marked expression of the demoted P argument or the multi-verbal strategy, with the demoted P as a core argument of the repeated verb root. This task is beyond the scope of this paper. 

\section{Lexical distribution and importance for lexicon building}\label{sec:lexicon_distribution}
Since agent foregrounding constructions overlap in many respects with properties associated with antipassive constructions, I draw on recent typological observations on antipassives to account for the distributional properties of the agent foregrounding morphological markers. \autoref{tab:predicates taking -…¢an and -ta…¢an} summarizes the distribution of the two‚Äëplace predicates that occur with \textit{‚Äë…¢an} and \textit{‚Äëta…¢an}.

First, note that these elements exhibit complementary and asymmetric distributional patterns. Specifically, predicates that are detransitivized by \textit{‚Äë…¢an} do not occur with \textit{‚Äëta…¢an}, and vice versa. Additionally, the shorter form shows a much wider distribution than the longer form, which is restricted to only a handful of predicates. Furthermore, although previous studies have not explicitly addressed the frequency of these suffixes, examples involving \textit{‚Äë…¢an} outnumber those with \textit{‚Äëta…¢an} in the available linguistic descriptions of Mocov√≠ \citep[see][]{gualdieri1998,Carrio2015,Carrio2015a}. Such an asymmetric distributional pattern is not unusual in languages that possess multiple antipassive marker \citep[see, for instance,][]{say2021,janic.witzlack-makarevich2021}. \citet[][]{serzant.etal2021}, furthermore, argue that frequency plays a role in the asymmetric distribution of antipassive markers coexisting in the same language. Specifically, they propose that ``the most frequent lexical input will correlate with the less marking on the verb and viceversa".

   Typologically, it is not unusual for ingestive verbs to show idiosyncratic behavior with respect to the valence alternation patterns they participate in and the transitivity profile of the verb lexicon \citep[see][]{Nichols2004}. In a typological survey on causative constructions \citet[][63-65]{Dixon2000}  concluded that ``drinking and eating are the transitive activities which people are most likely to make other people do, on every other continent". Similarly, for languages with an intransitive constraint on morphological causativization, \citet[][30-31]{Naess2009} and \citet[][48-54]{Amberber2009} observed more recently that not only are `eat' and `drink' flexible in terms of causativization, but so are ``other verbs referring to acts of  ingestion or consumption'', e.g.  `smoke' and `lick', as well as verbs of perception, e.g. `know'.  
   
   Lastly, given that the main focus of agent foregrounding constructions is the agent‚Äôs activity, it naturally follows that the animacy of the demoted P argument is not a relevant parameter of variation. Typological work has shown that the animacy of demoted P arguments plays a role in the variation observed in antipassives  \citep{say2021,zuniga.kittila2019}. However, the list of predicates in \autoref{tab:predicates taking -…¢an and -ta…¢an} and the examples discussed so far show that both animate and inanimate referents can serve as implied demoted P arguments, although one or the other may be semantically more appropriate depending on the predicate. For example, an animate P argument is implied with predicates such as \textit{-kin} `greet' or \textit{-la…æ} `send/order', while an inanimate participant is more suitable with predicates such as as \textit{-qawalat} `knead' or \textit{-pelo…°} `rake'. Other predicates allow both animate and inanimate P arguments depending on the discourse context, such as \textit{-t Éaq} `cut' or \textit{-lat} `leave'. Finally, note that the distribution of the two agent foregrounding markers cannot be determined by the animacy of the P argument, since both animate and inanimate referents may appear in \textit{-…¢an} and \textit{-ta…¢an} detransitivized constructions.

\section{Discourse distribution: a case study}{\label{sec:annotation-scheema}}
One aspect that has not received much attention in relation to agent foregrounding constructions concerns their discourse distribution. This section therefore examines the actual occurrence of \textit{‚Äë…¢an} and \textit{‚Äëta…¢an} in natural speech. The goal is purely descriptive: to determine how frequent the different types of agent foregrounding predicates are in discourse, and to identify any correspondence between predicate types and the expression of P arguments  \citep[for a similar example, see][]{haude.allassonniere-tang2023}. Although these questions are basic, such a quantitative investigation has not previously been carried out for valence‚Äëchanging operations in Mocov√≠ or in other Guaycuruan languages. There are, however, relevant studies on related topics, such as counts of lexical argument expression in Mocov√≠ \citep[see][]{califa2014} and Chaco Toba's discourse \citep[see][]{cuneo.messineo2019}.

The case study presented here is based on a single narrative discourse, used as a first step to complement the qualitative observations on the distribution and function of agent foregrounding constructions. The narrative has the characteristics of a monologue, in which the speaker recalls various past and present life events. Building on the clause types and patterns of argument expression described in section. An additional parameter is applied only to third‚Äëperson P arguments, identifying the determiner type used in nominal P‚Äëargument expressions. \section{Conclusions and outlook}{\label{sec:conclusions}}
This study has analyzed agent foregrounding constructions in Northern Chaco Mocov√≠ and their effects on the expression of core arguments in basic and derived two‚Äëplace predicates. It argues that agent foregrounding constructions, morphologically marked by \textit{-…¢an} or \textit{-ta…¢an}, primarily indicate the high prominence of the agent‚Äëlike argument, while secondarily affecting the expression of the P argument, which may be omitted, expressed as a bare noun, or marked as an oblique. Semantically, these strategies of P‚Äëargument demotion typically involve low definiteness and low discourse referentiality of the demoted nominal referents. Importantly, the paper also shows that the language employs multiverbal constructions to reintroduce the originally demoted P argument by repeating verb roots with identical semantics. This type of multiverbal strategy does not appear to be widespread cross‚Äëlinguistically and therefore warrants further typological research.

The lexical distribution of ‚Äë…¢an and ‚Äëta…¢an shows a complementary and asymmetrical pattern: most predicates participating in agent foregrounding are two‚Äëplace verbs, and ‚Äë…¢an is considerably more frequent than ‚Äëta…¢an. These observations align with typological work on antipassive‚Äëlike constructions and illustrate the semantic constraints that shape valence alternation in the language.

The study also highlights the importance of agent foregrounding in lexicon building. The detransitivized predicate provides the base for several derived nouns, particularly those denoting abstract entities associated with an agent‚Äôs activity. The nominalization process incorporates the agent as the possessor while eliminating reference to the demoted P argument, showing how valence alternation interacts with lexical derivation.

Finally, the discourse‚Äëbased analysis shows that agent foregrounding constructions are relatively infrequent but occur in specific functional contexts. When they appear, they contribute to the structuring of narrative information, especially by emphasizing the agent‚Äôs action and reducing the referential prominence of the P argument. These findings open a promising line of research for future work on valence‚Äëchanging mechanisms in Mocov√≠ and related Guaycuruan languages, as well as for broader typological investigations of antipassives and argument demotion.
